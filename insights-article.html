INSIGHTS / LITIGATION

When the case turns on a record no one has read carefully enough.

Joel Blanchet Founding Partner

Every litigator knows the feeling. The record is enormous. The deadline is real. And somewhere inside a few thousand pages is the detail that changes everything. The instinct is to move quickly — to skim, summarize, delegate. But speed without precision is just motion.

In our experience, the cases that turn on close reading are not rare. They are the norm. The transcript that contradicts the declaration. The metadata that disproves the timeline. The footnote in a regulatory filing that quietly concedes the point the other side is fighting hardest to deny.

None of this is dramatic. It is methodical. But it is also where cases are won — not in oral argument, not at trial, but in the months before, when someone reads what no one else bothered to read.

“The most important facts are rarely in the summary. They are in the attachments no one opened.”

The discipline of close reading

Close reading is not a personality trait. It is a practice. It requires protected time, structured review, and a willingness to revisit material that has already been flagged as unimportant. It means trusting the process over the instinct to move on.

At Blanchet, this is not an aspiration. It is the way the firm operates. Every case begins with a thorough accounting of the record — not delegated to software, not reduced to summaries, but read. The partners read. The associates read. The record is the case, and the case is in the record.

This approach is not efficient in the way that most firms define efficiency. It is efficient in the way that matters: it produces results. Cases resolve faster when the facts are understood earlier. Arguments sharpen when they rest on evidence that has been examined, not assumed.

What this means for clients

Clients who work with the firm often remark on a specific feeling: the sense that their lawyers know the case better than they do. That is not a coincidence. It is a consequence of the work.

When you retain counsel who has read the record completely, conversations change. Strategy sessions become substantive. Depositions become targeted. And settlement discussions begin from a position of genuine understanding — not posture, not bluster, but knowledge.
